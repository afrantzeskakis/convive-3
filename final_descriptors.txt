Vinegar: An aroma of acetic acid, identical to vinegar, which is sharp, sour, and piquant. If a wine smells like vinegar, it means there’s excessive volatile acidity (VA), usually from bacterial spoilage turning some of the wine to acetic acid. The odor is biting – like sniffing white wine vinegar or apple cider vinegar – and it’s a clear fault when strong (though many wines have a tiny amount of VA that can lift aromas without outright vinegar smell). Tasting it might also give a puckering sourness. Essentially, noticeable vinegar in wine signals that the wine has been exposed to oxygen and bacteria enough to partially spoil, and while it might work for salad dressing, it’s not pleasant in your glass.
Violet: A floral aroma evocative of violet flowers. It’s a sweet but subtle dark-floral scent, often found in certain red wines like Malbec, Nebbiolo, or Syrah. Smelling violets in wine means you detect a perfume akin to a bouquet of spring violets – slightly powdery, candy-like (think of parma violet candies), and very elegant. This note adds a beautiful floral lift to the wine’s aroma profile, often pairing with red and black fruit notes to increase the wine’s allure. It signals a refined bouquet, and is typically a positive descriptor indicating aromatic complexity.
Volcanic: A term used to describe a certain mineral quality that hints at volcanic soil origin – think sulfurous, smoky, or basalt-like notes. A wine described as volcanic might have a subtle aroma of pumice, lava rock, or gunpowder (a kind of flinty reduction combined with earthy smoke). It gives an impression of ash or burnt earth in a restrained way. This isn’t a common descriptor outside of context (like wines from Santorini or Mt. Etna, where tasters sometimes note a “volcanic minerality”). It contributes a distinctive sense of place – a rugged, smoky-stony nuance that can make the wine feel linked to volcanic soils. Essentially, calling a note volcanic is highlighting a specific kind of minerality and smokiness.
Walnut: A nutty aroma reminiscent of walnuts, particularly the slightly bitter, oily smell of walnut meat and skins. In wine, walnut notes often appear in oxidized or aged styles (like Sherry, Tawny Port, or mature whites), giving a dry, earthy nut scent. It’s less sweet than almond or hazelnut aromas; walnut has a more savory, woody nut character. This adds complexity, especially to fortified wines – think of the smell of cracked walnuts or walnut shells. Sometimes even in reds you might catch a walnut-skin note as the wine ages. It lends a mature, autumnal quality to the bouquet.
Wet dog: An unpleasant aroma often associated with cork-tainted wine (TCA contamination). It literally smells like a wet dog – musty, damp, and animal. People also compare it to the smell of soggy newspapers or a damp wool sweater. If a wine has wet dog odor, it’s almost certainly flawed, likely corked. The aroma overwhelms fruit and freshness, leaving the wine smelling dank and stale. It’s universally considered a fault; no amount of poetic spin makes “wet dog” a positive in a wine glass.
Wet wool: An aroma that smells like damp wool or a wet sweater – somewhat similar to wet dog but more on the fabric side than the animal side. This note is famously associated with some Chenin Blanc or Semillon wines (and also in minor TCA cases). In certain contexts like a Vouvray Chenin, a faint wet wool can be a varietal quirk and not necessarily a fault, providing an interesting lanolin-like character. It’s a subtle, neutral smell of wet fabric/hair that can accompany fruit aromas. However, if it’s strong, it could be off-putting. In balanced small amounts, some tasters find it adds complexity (descriptors like lanolin are used), but generally it’s not a highly sought aroma unless you’re deep into geeky wine description.
White flowers: A general term for delicate white-blossomed floral aromas, like those of jasmine, orange blossom, acacia, or hawthorn. If a wine is said to have white flowers on the nose, it means it carries a light, sweet floral perfume reminiscent of spring blossoms. This description avoids pinpointing a specific flower but conveys the impression of something like a mix of citrus blossom, honeysuckle, and similar subtle florals. White flower notes add elegance and finesse to a wine’s aroma, giving a sense of freshness and grace without the headiness of bigger floral notes (like rose). It’s commonly noted in fine whites and sparkling wines.
White pepper: A spice aroma akin to ground white peppercorn. It’s a more subtle peppery note than black pepper – slightly floral, mildly pungent, and a touch woody. In wine, white pepper is famously associated with Grüner Veltliner and some cool-climate Syrahs. It gives a delicate spiciness that tingles the nose lightly. Unlike the bold kick of black pepper, white pepper in wine feels more like a gentle sprinkle of spice that can enhance the overall aroma complexity. It adds a refined savory element, often meshing well with mineral and herb notes in the wine.
Yeasty: An aroma reminiscent of yeast or rising bread dough. It smells like a bakery in the morning – think of the scent of floury, fermenting dough or the whiff of a beer brewery. In wines, yeasty notes are common in those aged on lees (dead yeast cells), such as Champagne or Muscadet. It can be pleasant, giving bready, brioche-like nuances, and adding depth to the aroma. In young fermenting wine or if a wine hasn’t settled, a yeasty smell might also be present (like the smell of fresh beer or rising dough). Generally, yeasty in a finished wine refers to appealing bread, dough, or pastry crust notes that enrich the nose, signaling techniques like sur lie aging or bottle fermentation.
Youthful: Describes a wine that presents as young and undeveloped in bouquet and flavor. A youthful wine often has vibrant primary notes – lots of fresh fruit, maybe some floral and herb, but not much in the way of earthy or aged characters. Its tannins or acids might be a bit aggressive (since they haven’t mellowed) and the overall impression is lively and straightforward. Saying a wine is youthful implies it could benefit from more time to integrate and gain complexity, but it’s also a nod to the bright, exuberant qualities it has at that moment (fresh grapes, new oak vanilla if present, etc.). In appearance, a youthful red might be more purple-tinted, and a youthful white might be very pale with greenish hues. It’s essentially a wine in its early stage, proudly showing off its simple, fresh charms.
Zesty: An adjective for a wine that is lively and tangy, often with pronounced acidity or citrus-driven flavors. A zesty wine gives a sensation akin to biting into a lemon or sipping something with a spicy kick – it’s refreshing, energetic, and maybe slightly prickly (if lightly effervescent or very acidic). Typically used for whites or rosés, zesty means the wine has a citrus peel or spice brightness that enlivens your palate. Think of Sauvignon Blanc with that grapefruit zing or a young Riesling with limey snap – they feel zesty. The term conveys that the wine is anything but dull; it has pep and brightness, leaving your mouth feeling clean and awakened by the experience.
