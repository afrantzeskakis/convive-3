Buttery: Having an aroma or flavor evocative of butter or cream. A buttery wine often gives a smooth, rich mouthfeel and a scent like melted butter or buttered toast. This is commonly seen in some Chardonnay that has undergone malolactic fermentation, resulting in a creamy, dairy-like note. It makes the wine taste lush and round, with a flavor hinting at fresh butter or even butterscotch in some cases.
Gamey: An aroma reminiscent of game or wild meats – slightly bloody, leathery, or like raw meat. A gamey note gives the wine a rustic, savory character, as if there’s a hint of smoked meat or cured sausage in the aroma. It’s often found in certain red wines like Rhône reds or Burgundy, contributing a wild, animal undertone that adds complexity (in moderation) to the fruit flavors.
Garlic: A pungent aroma akin to raw garlic or garlic powder, sharp and sulfurous. In wine, a true garlic note is usually a flaw arising from certain sulfur compounds (mercaptans). It introduces an odd savory-acrid scent that can overwhelm the nose, reminiscent of chopped garlic – generally considered an off-aroma, as it detracts from fruit or floral notes.
Garrigue: A term (borrowed from French) referring to the mix of wild resinous herbs found in Mediterranean scrubland – such as thyme, rosemary, lavender, and juniper. In wine, garrigue describes that fragrant herbal medley aroma. A wine with garrigue notes might smell like dried herbs, earthy shrub, and pine, giving a distinctly southern French countryside character – savory, herbal, and slightly floral all at once.
Geranium: A pungent floral/herbal note resembling crushed geranium leaves or the scent of geranium flower oil. It tends to be a sharp, green floral smell. In wine, a geranium note is often a flaw caused by the wine additive sorbic acid, which can metabolize into a geranium-like aroma. It is generally considered unpleasant – like a mix of raw greens and faint flowers – and is not a typical positive descriptor.
Ginger: A spice note smelling of fresh ginger root or ginger snaps. It has a warm, gently piquant aroma – lightly spicy, citrusy, and sweet. In some wines, especially those that are aged or botrytized, you might catch a ginger hint that adds zesty spice to the flavor profile. It can come across as fresh ginger’s subtle heat or candied ginger’s sweet spice, contributing liveliness and warmth.
Gooseberry: A tart green fruit aroma strongly associated with Sauvignon Blanc wines. Gooseberry smells like a mix of sour grape, kiwi, and green tart berries – vibrant and zesty. In wine it presents as a sharp, tangy fruit note with a hint of grassiness. It gives an impression of green acidity and is a classic descriptor for New Zealand Sauvignon Blanc, lending a fresh but pungent fruit character.
Grapefruit: A citrus aroma reminiscent of pink or yellow grapefruit, including its tangy juice and slight bitterness. This note is bright and zesty – smelling it in wine is like cutting open a fresh grapefruit. It brings both citrusy sweetness and pithy bitterness. Often found in white wines (like certain Sauvignon Blancs or Rieslings), grapefruit notes make the wine feel refreshing and crisp, with a bit of a bitter citrus kick on the finish.
Grapey: A simple, direct aroma that literally smells like grapes or grape juice. It’s the scent you get from freshly crushed table grapes or grape candy – sweet, purple-fruit and straightforward. Describing a wine as grapey usually means it retains a lot of primary grape flavor (common in young wines or certain varieties like Muscat), giving the impression of biting into a sweet grape or drinking grape juice.
Graphite: A mineral aroma similar to the smell of pencil lead (which is graphite) or a struck flint. It’s a clean, sharp stony scent with a hint of something metallic. In wines like Cabernet Sauvignon or Bordeaux blends, graphite often describes that refined pencil-shavings note on the nose. It lends a sense of minerality and sophistication to the aroma, as if you smelled a freshly sharpened pencil in the glass.
Grassy: An aroma reminiscent of fresh-cut grass or hay. It’s a green, herbaceous scent that can make a wine smell like a spring meadow or a just-mowed lawn. Grassy notes are common in certain white wines (like some Sauvignon Blancs) and give a fresh, outdoorsy character. This can be pleasant in balance, adding freshness, though if too strong it might make the wine seem overly green or vegetal.
Green: A general descriptor for flavors or aromas that suggest under-ripeness or plant-like characteristics. A “green” wine might have notes of bell pepper, grass, herbal leafiness, or unripe fruit. On the palate it can come across as slightly bitter or lacking sweetness, similar to biting into an unripe fruit or green vegetable. This term indicates the wine tastes a bit raw or undeveloped in its fruit profile.
Green bell pepper: The distinct aroma of fresh green bell pepper (capsicum). It’s a crunchy, vegetal smell – think of sniffing a sliced bell pepper, which is herbaceous and slightly sweet. In wines, especially Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot or Carménère, a green bell pepper note can appear due to compounds called pyrazines. It adds a savory, vegetal dimension to the wine; in small amounts it can be interesting, but if dominant it may indicate under-ripe grapes, making the wine smell very green.
Green tea: A delicate aroma akin to brewed green tea or tea leaves. It’s lightly herbaceous, slightly nutty, and has a subtle dried grass character. In wine, a green tea note might show up in fine aged whites or light reds, contributing a nuanced leafy or tannic scent. It’s generally mild and adds complexity, reminiscent of the gentle, soothing smell of green tea rather than bold herbal tones.
Guava: A tropical fruit aroma reminiscent of fresh guava pulp. It smells sweet, fragrant, and tropical – like a mix of pear, mango, and light citrus all in one. In wine, guava notes are often found in aromatic white varieties (or New World whites) giving an exotic, lush fruit character. This note makes the wine’s aroma profile feel tropical and ripe, as if a hint of guava nectar were present.
Gunflint: A term for a specific smoky-mineral scent, like the smell of flint stone striking or gunpowder. It’s a steely, smoky rock aroma often noted in certain white wines (like flinty Chablis or Sauvignon Blanc from Sancerre). Gunflint gives a subtle smoke and stone character – imagine the faint spark smell from striking two flints together. It contributes to the perceived minerality of a wine, adding complexity beyond fruit aromas.
Hard: Describes a wine that tastes overly tannic or acidic in an unyielding way, lacking softness. A hard wine strikes the palate with force – the tannins might feel aggressive and drying, or the acids too sharp. This makes the texture feel rough or the flavors unapproachable. Such a wine may need time to soften, but at the moment it comes off as rigid or stern, without enough fruit or roundness to balance those firm elements.
Hawthorn: A delicate floral note similar to the blossoms of the hawthorn tree. Hawthorn flowers have a light, sweet, and slightly musky white-flower aroma. In wine, a hawthorn note often appears in certain white wines (and is commonly cited in French tasting) imparting a gentle hedgerow floral character. It enhances the bouquet with an airy spring-blossom scent that is subtle and pleasant.
Hay: An aroma of dry hay or straw, giving a wine an earthy, sun-dried grass character. It’s reminiscent of walking into a barn with dried hay bales – slightly sweet, herbaceous, and rustic. Hay notes can be found in some white wines or lighter reds, contributing a countryside-like nuance. It can lend an impression of warmth and maturity, as if the fruit is intertwined with dried field grass.
Hazelnut: A nutty aroma reminiscent of roasted hazelnuts. It’s toasty, slightly sweet-nutty, and can also recall the smell of praline or nougat. In wine, hazelnut notes often develop in whites aged in oak or on lees (such as some Chardonnays or Champagnes), giving a delicate nutty richness. It adds depth to the aroma, as if a hint of roasted nuts or hazelnut spread were present in the background.
Heavy: Describes a wine that feels very full, weighty, or high in alcohol to the point of being out of balance. A heavy wine can come across as plodding or tiring on the palate – you might feel the alcohol warmth strongly or the flavors might be very dense and overbearing. It’s akin to a dish that is too rich. The wine lacks lift or freshness, instead giving an impression of thickness or excessive weight, which can dull the drinking experience.
Herbaceous: Having aromas or flavors that are green and herb-like, reminiscent of herbs, leaves, or green vegetation. An herbaceous wine might smell of fresh-cut grass, basil, oregano, green pepper, or other leafy greens. This character often comes from compounds in certain grapes (like Sauvignon Blanc or Cabernet family varietals). Depending on intensity, herbaceous notes can add desirable freshness and complexity or, if dominant, make the wine seem vegetal or under-ripe.
Hibiscus: A floral aroma similar to hibiscus flowers or hibiscus tea. It’s a soft, sweet floral with a slight berry-like and tart edge (as hibiscus used in tea has a cranberry-like aspect). In wine, hibiscus notes are not very common but might be found in some aromatic profiles, contributing a fragrant red-floral tone. It adds an exotic bloom character, as if a subtle waft of floral fruit tea or tropical flower is present.
Hollow: Describes a wine that lacks mid-palate flavor or depth – it starts and finishes with some impression, but there’s an empty gap in the middle. When tasting a hollow wine, you might notice initial fruit or acid, then a notable absence of flavor in the center of your mouth, and then perhaps a bit of tannin or heat in the finish. It feels as though the wine has a hole in its flavor profile, making it seem less complete or satisfying.
Honey: A sweet aroma reminiscent of honey or honeycomb. It evokes the scent of wildflower honey – floral, nectar-like, and richly sweet. In wine, honey notes are common in late-harvest or botrytized wines (like Sauternes or Riesling Auslese), but can also appear in aged whites. It lends a luxurious sweetness on the nose, even if the wine is dry. This note makes the bouquet feel warm and golden, as if a drizzle of honey were mixed in.
Honeysuckle: A floral aroma akin to honeysuckle blossoms – sweet, nectar-like, and delicate. It smells like spring evenings: a soft, perfumed sweetness that isn’t overly heavy. In wine, honeysuckle is often noted in fragrant white varieties (like Viognier or some muscat-based wines), adding an enticing flower perfume. It contributes a fresh yet sweet floral character, much like sniffing a honeysuckle vine in bloom.
Horse blanket (sweaty saddle): A rustic aroma often associated with Brettanomyces that smells like a well-worn leather saddle or a horse blanket. It’s an animalistic, sweaty odor with elements of leather, musk, and barnyard. In small amounts, some find it can add old-world complexity (a kind of rough, outdoorsy charm), but if strong it is usually considered a flaw. Essentially, it’s the smell of a sweaty horse tack room – a mix of dried sweat, leather, and animal – and can greatly overshadow fruit if prevalent.
Horse manure: A pungent barnyard aroma that specifically recalls the smell of horse droppings or a manure pile. It’s an earthy, fecal smell – generally unwelcome in wine. This odor indicates a strong Brettanomyces influence or spoilage. While terms like “barnyard” might encompass mild touches of animal musk, horse manure is more intense and clearly off-putting, as it suggests the wine’s aroma has been overtaken by foul, farm waste-like scents.
Hot: Describes the burning sensation on the palate from high alcohol. A hot wine makes your mouth and throat feel warm or even sting slightly, similar to a sip of spirit. This heat can overshadow flavors, giving a impression of peppery spice or solvent. When a wine is described as hot, it generally means the alcohol is not well integrated – you feel the alcohol disproportionately, which can detract from the overall balance and pleasure of the wine.
Incense: A spicy, resinous aroma reminiscent of burning incense sticks (such as frankincense or myrrh). It has a sweet smoke, churchlike fragrance – think aromatic resins, sandalwood, and spice. In some full-bodied or aged wines, especially those with rich oak influence, you might detect an incense-like note. This gives the wine an exotic, smoky-sweet perfume, adding a layer of complexity that feels almost spiritual or meditative in character.
Iodine: A briny, medicinal aroma similar to tincture of iodine or sea air. It’s a scent that hints at salt, metal, and medicine cabinet. In wine, iodine notes sometimes appear in very mature reds or certain terroir-specific wines (like some from coastal or volcanic soils). It can give a savory, almost bloody/mineral nuance (people also liken it to seashore or oyster shell). A touch of iodine can add intrigue, but too much might seem medicinal or odd.
Iris: A delicate floral note akin to iris flowers. The aroma of iris (especially the orris root derived from it) is soft, powdery-floral with a slight earthy woodiness. In wine, an iris note would contribute a gentle, elegant floral perfume – not as overtly sweet as rose, nor as citrusy as orange blossom, but a refined floral hint. It’s not very common to identify specifically, but when present it elevates the fragrance with a classy floral tone.
Jalapeño: A green, spicy aroma similar to the smell of a fresh cut jalapeño pepper (without necessarily the chili heat on the palate). It has a distinctive vegetal spice note – think green pepper with a slight kick. In wines, this can be noted in some Sauvignon Blancs or Cabernet Francs that have pronounced pyrazines, lending a peppery green chili character. It adds a fiery herbaceous touch to the aroma, though the wine itself isn’t “spicy” in terms of capsaicin heat.
Jammy: Describes a wine with very ripe, sweet fruit flavors reminiscent of jam or preserves. A jammy wine is bursting with cooked-fruit sweetness – think strawberry jam, blackberry preserves, or plum compote. The fruit is intense and concentrated, often as a result of very ripe grapes or hot-climate growing conditions. While jammy notes can make a wine feel generous and fruit-forward, if overdone the wine may lack freshness or structure, coming off as simple or cloyingly fruity.
Jasmine: A floral aroma akin to jasmine flowers. It’s a sweet, heady white-flower scent often associated with night-blooming jasmine – very fragrant and perfumed. In wine, jasmine notes can appear in aromatic whites or certain sweet wines, lending an exotic, floral elegance. It adds a lovely perfume element to the bouquet, as if a few jasmine petals were steeped in the wine, imparting their intense, flowery sweetness.
Kirsch: The aroma of kirschwasser (a cherry brandy), essentially the scent of distilled cherries – powerful, sweet, and alcoholic cherry fragrance. Kirsch in wine tasting refers to a candied, spirit-like black cherry note. It often appears in descriptions of rich reds (like some Grenache-based wines or Barolo), where the cherry aroma is so ripe and concentrated it’s reminiscent of cherry liqueur. This note gives a combination of sweet dark cherry and a hint of almond/boozy warmth, adding depth to the fruit character.
Lavender: A floral-herbal aroma reminiscent of lavender buds. It’s sweet, purple-floral but also slightly camphorous and herbal. In wine, lavender notes might be found in some Mediterranean reds or aromatic whites, contributing a Provence-like scent. The presence of lavender gives the wine a soothing yet vibrant floral character, as if a touch of lavender oil or dried lavender flowers were in the bouquet.
Lean: Describes a wine that is light-bodied and lacks plushness, often with higher acidity and not much richness. A lean wine feels taut and maybe a bit thin – it emphasizes crispness or structure over ripe fruit. On the palate, it may come across as narrowly focused (perhaps more mineral or green notes, fewer sweet fruit flavors). While lean wines can be refreshing and elegant, if taken to an extreme they might seem undernourished or lacking flavor generosity.
Leather: An aroma reminiscent of leather material – like an old leather jacket or a saddle. It’s a warm, earthy, somewhat animal scent with a bit of dryness. Leather notes commonly develop in aged red wines (like mature Rioja or Bordeaux) or wines with Brettanomyces influence. A touch of leather can add a sense of rustic warmth and complexity, giving the impression of old books or fine leather furniture integrated with the wine’s fruit.
Lemon: A citrus aroma and flavor like that of a fresh lemon. It carries the zesty brightness and tang of lemon juice along with the fragrant quality of lemon peel. In wine, lemon notes contribute a lively, tart citrus character (common in many white wines, from Chablis to Riesling). It makes the wine feel crisp and refreshing – as if a squeeze of lemon were enhancing the flavors. Sometimes one might distinguish lemon zest (the aromatic, oily peel) versus lemon juice (the sour part); both aspects add to a wine’s vibrant acidity and aroma.
Lemongrass: An aroma akin to fresh lemongrass, a citrusy herb used in Asian cuisine. It has a distinctive lemon-citrus scent with green herbal undertones. In wine tasting, lemongrass notes might appear in certain Sauvignon Blancs or other zesty whites, contributing a complex citrus-herbal character. It makes the wine’s aroma interesting by adding that tangy lemon plus light herbal grass combination, which comes off as both refreshing and exotic.
Licorice: A note that resembles black licorice candy or anise licorice root. It’s sweet and herbal-spicy at the same time, with a recognizable aniseed fragrance. In wines, especially some Italian reds or Syrahs, a licorice note can emerge, giving a sweet-woody spice nuance. It deepens the flavor profile by adding that candied fennel flavor – a bit earthy, a bit sweet, and slightly bitter – much like the taste of a licorice twist.
Lilac: A floral aroma similar to lilac blossoms. Lilacs have a sweet, enchanting springtime scent that is both floral and slightly powdery. In wine, lilac notes may be subtle but can appear in aromatic whites or light reds, imparting a gentle purple-flower fragrance. This adds a pretty, fragrant top note to the wine’s bouquet, as if a breeze of lilac from a garden is passing through the glass.
Lime: A citrus aroma/taste reminiscent of fresh lime juice or lime zest. It has a sharp, tangy profile with a slightly floral-citrus peel aroma. Lime notes in wine (often in Rieslings, Sauvignon Blancs, or Australian Semillon) give a punchy citrus character that’s very vibrant. It contributes to high acidity perception – making the wine feel extra crisp – and leaves a refreshing, tart citrus impression similar to squeezing a lime wedge.
Linden (lime blossom): A floral aroma of linden tree blossoms (often called lime blossom, though unrelated to the citrus fruit). Linden flowers smell sweet, green-floral, and somewhat honeyed. In wine, a linden blossom note imparts a gentle, spring-like floral character (common in some Rieslings or Loire whites). It’s an elegant, soft perfume suggesting a mix of flowers and faint honey, adding refinement to the aroma.
Lit match: The distinctive smell right after striking a match – essentially a sulfurous, smoky note. In wine, a “lit match” aroma typically comes from a bit of free sulfur (sulfur dioxide) or certain reductive processes, and can be found in some Chardonnays or other wines that see controlled oxygen exposure. It smells slightly of burnt sulfur and flint. In small amounts, some tasters enjoy it as a nuance (referred to as struck match or gunflint, especially in Burgundian-style Chardonnay), but if too strong it can be acrid. It adds a flinty, smoky accent to the scent profile.
Lively: Describes a wine that feels energetic on the palate, often thanks to good acidity or slight effervescence. A lively wine has a refreshing, spirited character – it makes your taste buds perk up. You might sense a crisp tang or a subtle fizz that gives a prickle. Overall, calling a wine lively means it comes across as fresh, vibrant, and full of life, rather than dull or heavy.
Lychee: A tropical fruit aroma typical of lychee fruit, which is very fragrant and sweet. It’s like a combination of grapes and roses with a touch of melon – a unique perfumed fruit scent. Lychee is famously noted in Gewürztraminer and some Muscat wines, giving them an exotic, floral-fruit character. This aroma makes the wine stand out with a lush, almost perfumed sweetness, as if rosewater and pear were mixed together, contributing to a rich aromatic profile.
Mango: A tropical fruit aroma reminiscent of ripe mango. It’s lush, sweet, and a bit musky, with a rich yellow-fruit scent. In wine, mango notes often appear in aromatic whites or sweet wines, adding an exotic, ripe fruit character. Smelling a mango note in wine is like catching the scent of a juicy, soft mango at peak ripeness – it imparts a sense of tropical richness and sweetness to the flavor profile.
Maple syrup: A rich, sweet aroma similar to the scent of maple syrup. It has a caramelized sugar quality with woody undertones (since maple syrup comes from tree sap). In wine, a maple syrup note might emerge in certain heavily botrytized dessert wines or long-aged sweet wines, giving a deep caramel-sugar and slight smoky wood sweetness. It adds a decadent, dessert-like element to the bouquet – as if a hint of pancake syrup or burnt sugar were present.
