Marmalade: An aroma reminiscent of orange marmalade, which is a mix of sweet citrus and bitter orange peel. It carries candied orange notes with a hint of zest bitterness and sugar. In wine, marmalade is often noted in sweet or aged whites (especially those with botrytis) where citrus peel and honeyed sweetness combine. It imparts a complex citrus character – both fruity and slightly tangy-bitter – much like tasting orange jam with peel included.
Marzipan: The sweet aroma of almond paste (marzipan), which smells like sugary almonds – nutty and confectionary. It’s a bit like sweet almond candy or almond extract. In wine, marzipan notes can be found in some sparkling wines or whites aged on lees, contributing a dessert-like nutty sweetness. It enriches the aroma with a patisserie quality (imagine an almond pastry or cake icing scent), adding to the wine’s complexity especially as it ages.
Mature: Describes a wine that has reached a developed stage of its life, where primary fruitiness may have faded and more complex, secondary and tertiary notes have emerged. A mature wine often shows notes of dried fruits, nuts, earth, or leather that weren’t present when young. On the palate it tends to be smoother, with tannins softened. Saying a wine is mature implies it’s at or near its optimal drinking window, displaying harmonious, evolved flavors (as opposed to fresh, youthful ones).
Meaty: An aroma or flavor that is reminiscent of raw or cooked meat. It can evoke the savoriness of cured meats, broth, or even the slight bloodiness of a rare steak. In some bold red wines (like Syrah or Mourvèdre), a meaty note adds a savory depth – you might feel as though there’s a hint of beef bouillon or charcuterie in the glass. This hearty characteristic rounds out the fruit with a robust, umami quality.
Mellow: Describes a wine that is soft, smooth, and lacking any harsh edges. A mellow wine has gentle flavors – nothing jarring or overly acidic/tannic. It comes across as relaxed on the palate, often as a result of age or good balance. The term suggests an easy-drinking nature: the wine’s components have integrated, its intensity is moderate, and it gives a soothing, pleasant experience without challenging the senses.
Melon: A fresh fruit note resembling the smell of melons, often honeydew or cantaloupe. It’s a mild, green-sweet aroma that is less sharp than citrus and not as overtly tropical as mango – more of a clean, dewy fruit scent. In wine, melon notes (like honeydew melon) frequently appear in lighter white wines (such as some Pinot Grigio or Chablis), providing a gentle fruitiness. This aroma makes the wine seem refreshing and approachable, with a hint of summer fruit softness.
Menthol: An aroma akin to mentholated mint or eucalyptus – cooling, minty, and slightly medicinal. It’s the sort of nose-tingling fresh scent you get from menthol cough drops or a crushed eucalyptus leaf. In wines, menthol notes often appear in some Cabernet Sauvignon or Shiraz, contributing a cooling herbal lift. It can make the wine smell a bit like mint or camphor, adding complexity and a sense of freshness despite the wine’s richness.
Metallic: A smell or taste that is reminiscent of metal or coins, like touching your tongue to iron or copper. It can also evoke the scent of blood (which is high in iron). In wine, a metallic note might present as a faint taste of iron or a tinny aroma. Sometimes it’s linked to high iron in soil or slight oxidation. In small amounts, it can add a mineral edge, but if strong it’s usually a flaw, making the wine taste a bit like metal or rust, which is generally unpleasant.
Mineral: A broad term describing aromas/flavors that resemble rocks, wet stone, chalk, or other non-organic elements. A mineral note in wine isn’t fruity or herbal – it’s more like the smell of wet slate, limestone, flint, or even a saline, chalky tone. This can give a wine a sense of place (terroir), often noted in Old World wines. Minerality often comes across as a stony, earthy freshness – imagine sniffing a handful of river pebbles or limestone after rain. It adds complexity and is typically subtle, underlying the fruit with a steely or earthy foundation.
Mint: An aroma of fresh mint leaves. It’s herbaceous, cool, and sweetly pungent, like smelling a crushed spearmint or peppermint leaf. In some wines (for instance, Coonawarra Cabernet or certain Chilean reds), a mint note can be present, lending a refreshing lift to the bouquet. It adds a cooling herbal character that can balance rich fruit, making the wine smell slightly green in a pleasant, invigorating way, as if garnished with a spring of mint.
Molasses: A deep, sweet aroma similar to dark molasses or treacle – essentially a rich, brown-sugar syrup scent. It has notes of burnt sugar, toffee, and a bit of smoky bitterness. In wine, molasses notes might appear in very ripe, port-like wines or extremely concentrated sweet wines, giving a thick, sugarcane sweetness with a hint of char. It adds a sense of heaviness and richness, contributing to a dessert-like profile (imagine the scent of brown sugar caramelizing in a pan).
Monolithic: Describes a wine that tastes one-dimensional, lacking complexity or development of flavors. A monolithic wine presents basically one dominant flavor (often fruit or oak) that carries through without variation. It doesn’t evolve much from start to finish on your palate. This term suggests the wine, while perhaps bold, is straightforward and somewhat simplistic – all its elements seem fused into a single block of flavor, without layers or nuance.
Mulberry: A dark berry fruit note akin to mulberries, which taste like a cross between blackberry and grape with a slight floral edge. In wine terms, mulberry suggests a juicy, dark berry character that isn’t as tart as raspberry nor as sweet as ripe blackberry – something in between, often slightly plummy and rustic. This note might be found in some red wines from warm climates, contributing to the dark fruit spectrum with a unique berry tone. It adds to the wine’s fruitiness in a soft, rounded way.
Mushroom: An earthy aroma reminiscent of fresh mushrooms or forest floor fungus. It has a damp, woodsy scent with a bit of savory earth. In wine, mushroom notes often develop with age (particularly in Pinot Noir or mature Champagne) or can indicate certain terroir. This adds an autumnal, truffle-like depth to the aroma. A touch of mushroom can make a wine smell more complex and aged – akin to the smell of a misty forest or a basket of wild mushrooms – and is generally a positive trait in aged fine wines.
Musky: A descriptor for a subtle animalistic or perfumed aroma that is slightly sweet but also earthy or animal. Musky notes can be like the scent of musk perfume (which is derived from animal musk) – it’s a deep, body-like sweet odor. In wine, a musky note might come from very ripe grapes or certain aromatic varieties, giving a sensual, earthy-floral nuance. It can make the wine’s bouquet feel heavier or more exotic, but if overdone it might be perceived as funky or cloying.
Musty (wet cardboard): A damp, stale aroma often compared to wet cardboard, moldy basement, or a mildewed cloth. Musty indicates the wine likely has been affected by cork taint (TCA) or poor storage, and it smells like cardboard that got wet and grew mold – dull, dusty, and earthy in a bad way. This off-aroma drowns out fruit and is considered a fault. If a wine is musty, it’s like sniffing an old damp newspaper or a dank cellar, lacking freshness and appeal.
Nail polish remover: A sharp, solvent-like aroma identical to acetone (the main chemical in many nail polish removers). It smells like a mix of artificial fruit and strong solvent/varnish. In wine, this indicates the presence of volatile acidity (particularly ethyl acetate) beyond the normal level. It’s generally a flaw if strong – the wine’s scent stings the nose a bit like sniffing actual nail polish remover. You might catch a whiff of something vaguely like glue or paint thinner combined with overripe fruit. In small doses it can add complexity, but if you distinctly smell nail polish remover, the wine is considered to have too much VA.
Nectarine: A stone fruit aroma similar to that of a ripe nectarine (which is quite like peach but sometimes a bit more floral and tart). It smells of sweet, juicy orange-yellow flesh with a slight zesty skin note. In wine, nectarine notes (common in some Viogniers, Rieslings, or Chardonnays) give a luscious fruit character that’s in between peach and apricot. It adds to the wine’s fruit profile with a summery sweetness and a hint of citrusy lift.
Nervy: Describes a wine high in acidity that’s exciting and edgy on the palate. A nervy wine has a certain tension – a brisk, electric feel that comes from pronounced acidity or minerality. It makes your palate sit up, almost on edge (in a good way), as if the wine is tightly wound. People often use “nervy” for young Rieslings or cool-climate wines where the brightness borders on being piercing, but also gives the wine a vivid, dynamic character.
Nettles: An aroma of stinging nettles or nettle tea – green, herbal, and slightly sharp. It’s somewhat like crushed green leaves with a faint peppery edge. In wine, nettle notes are particularly associated with Sauvignon Blanc (especially from New Zealand or the Loire), contributing to the green spectrum of its aromas. It reinforces a wild, herbal freshness, as if a bunch of nettle or cut weeds were part of the bouquet, adding pungency and greenery.
Nutmeg: A spice aroma similar to ground nutmeg – warm, sweet-spicy, and a bit woody. In wines, nutmeg notes might come from oak aging or complexity in certain rich wines, giving a gentle baking spice character. It’s not as sharp as clove or cinnamon; rather, it’s softer and rounder. The presence of a nutmeg note adds a cozy spiciness (think holiday eggnog or custard sprinkled with nutmeg) which can complement fruit flavors nicely.
Nutty: A general term for aromas or flavors reminiscent of nuts. A nutty wine might evoke almonds, hazelnuts, walnuts, or brazil nuts depending on context. This usually comes from oxidative aging (like in Sherry or some aged whites) or lees contact. It can manifest as a roasted almond note, a walnut skin bitterness, or a sweet hazelnut nuance. Nutty elements add complexity – they lend a wine a savory, toasty aspect as if a handful of nuts were mixed in, often accompanying flavors of butter, caramel, or oxidation in certain styles.
Oaky: Indicates the presence of oak-derived aromas and flavors – as one would expect from wine aged in oak barrels. An oaky wine often smells of things like vanilla, cedar, toast, or baking spices due to the oak. You might also sense a bit of woodsmoke or sawdust. On the palate, it can add tannin and a creamy texture. Essentially, oaky describes that wood-influenced character: think of the scent of a new oak barrel, the sweetness of vanilla, the spice of toasted wood, and sometimes a charred or coconut note (from American oak). When balanced, oak can give structure and complexity; if overdone, the wine might taste predominantly of wood.
Oily: Describes both a texture and a subtle aroma – like vegetable oil or lanolin. An oily wine has a viscous, almost slippery mouthfeel, often seen in certain white wines (like some Alsace Pinot Gris or aged Semillon). Aromatically, oily can refer to a faint smell of oil or wax (akin to linseed oil or machine oil, but very subtle and usually not unpleasant). It gives the wine a weighty, coating sensation on the palate, and sometimes a slight sheen or gloss to its flavor, indicating high glycerol or specific grape traits.
Olive: A savory aroma reminiscent of briny olives, particularly black olives. It carries an earthy, salty tang along with a dark, almost meaty fruit scent. In wines like Syrah or some Southern Italian reds, you might get a black olive note that adds complexity. It’s not fruity in a sweet sense – it’s more of a leathery, salty, fermented fruit smell. This can lend the wine a distinctly Mediterranean character, bringing a nice savory counterpoint to any ripe fruit flavors.
Onion: A pungent aroma akin to raw onion or onion skin. Like garlic, an obvious onion smell in wine is generally a flaw related to sulfur compounds (mercaptans). It’s unusual but if present, it’s typically negative – the wine might remind one of oniony BO or vegetable sulfur. In rare positive contexts, very slight onion skin could describe a nuance in oxidation (like Sherry), but as a fresh onion note, it’s not considered desirable.
Opulent: Describes a wine that is lavish, richly textured, and generous in flavor. An opulent wine often has a full body, high glycerin, and luxurious, ripe fruit that gives a sense of sweetness (even if the wine is dry). Drinking it feels like indulging – it’s velvety, mouth-filling, and maybe a bit heady. Opulent wines typically have layers of flavor (ripe fruit, spice, oak) that all come across as plush and showy, making the tasting experience feel decadent.
Orange: A citrus aroma or flavor like that of an orange – sweet and bright. In some wines, especially aromatic whites or orange wines (white wines made with skin contact), you can detect an orange note, be it like orange juice, orange zest, or blossoms. This adds a pleasant citrusy sweetness and zing. It’s usually accompanied by floral aspects (orange blossom) or candied peel nuances if in sweeter wines. Overall, an orange note lifts the wine with a sunny, citrus fruit character.
Orange blossom: A floral aroma of orange blossoms, the flowers of the orange tree. This scent is intensely sweet, airy, and citrusy-floral. In wine, orange blossom notes are often found in fragrant whites (like Muscat, Albariño, or Viognier), giving a perfume that’s both floral and suggestive of the citrus fruit. It’s an enchanting note that adds elegance – imagine walking by a blooming orange tree on a warm night; that heady, sweet fragrance is what orange blossom brings to a wine’s bouquet.
Overripe: Describes flavors or aromas that suggest the grapes were extremely ripe (even too ripe) when picked. An overripe wine tends to have very lush, sometimes cooked fruit character with lower acidity. You might taste stewed fruits, jam, or even raisin-like notes rather than fresh fruit. Aromas could lean toward prune, fig, or syrupy sweetness. While ripeness gives intensity, overripe implies it’s a bit too far – the wine can feel heavy, lacking freshness, and may have a higher alcohol or a slight porty quality from those super-mature grapes.
Oxidized: Describes a wine that has been exposed to oxygen more than intended, resulting in specific aroma/flavor changes. An oxidized wine often smells nutty, sherry-like or like bruised apples, and the fruit flavors will be dulled or turned to more dried fruit notes. White wines darken towards gold/brown and gain caramel, nut, or apple cider characteristics; red wines can turn tawny and taste flat, with dried fruit and nuts. In general, oxidized indicates the wine has lost its fresh aromas and has taken on an “old,” somewhat stale character (similar to how a sliced apple turns brown and smells different). In most cases (except deliberately oxidized styles like Sherry), this is considered a fault.
Papaya: A tropical fruit aroma reminiscent of ripe papaya. It’s sweet and mellow, with a scent that’s a mix of melon and mango but creamier and less tangy. In wine, papaya notes might appear in very ripe, tropical white wines or some New World Chardonnays/Chenins, giving a soft tropical-fruit character. It adds to the exotic profile of the wine, making it smell like a blend of tropical fruit salad – the papaya element is that gentle, sweet, almost musky tropical note.
Passion fruit: A vibrant tropical aroma strongly associated with some Sauvignon Blancs (especially from New Zealand). Passion fruit smells tangy, exotic, and very fruity – like a mix of citrus, guava, and mango with a sharp tropical punch. In wine, this note makes the aroma jump out as very fruity and fresh, sometimes slightly tart as well. It contributes a bold tropical character that can be quite distinctive, adding to the wine’s allure with something that smells like tropical juice.
Peach: A stone fruit aroma like that of a ripe peach. It’s sweet, juicy, and a little floral. In many white wines (e.g., Riesling, Moscato, some Chardonnays), peach notes are common and very appealing – it’s like the smell you get when biting into a ripe yellow peach, dripping with juice. This note gives the wine a friendly, luscious fruit character. Depending on ripeness, it could range from the fresher scent of a just-ripe peach to the almost syrupy note of canned peaches in heavy syrup.
Pear: A fruit aroma reminiscent of fresh pear (typically green or yellow pears like Bartlett). It’s a gentle, sweet fragrance with a slight floral aspect and less acidity than apple. Many lighter white wines and some rosés exhibit pear notes, contributing to an easy-going fruity profile. It smells like biting into a ripe, juicy pear – subtle, with honeyed sweetness and a mild perfume. This adds a smooth orchard-fruit element to the wine’s flavor, often making it seem supple and approachable.
Pear (dried): The scent of dried pear or pear chips – a concentrated pear aroma that’s sweeter and a bit caramelized compared to fresh pear. Dried pear notes in wine often appear in richer whites or dessert wines, giving a candied or compote-like fruit character. It’s as if the pear’s essence is intensified, with some honeyed and lightly brown sugar tones. This adds depth to the wine’s fruitiness, leaning more towards a dessert-like, developed fruit profile rather than fresh.
Peardrop: The aroma of pear drop candies, a type of hard candy that smells like artificial pear and banana. It’s a distinctly sweet, estery scent – very confectionary and reminiscent of some young Beaujolais or certain fermentation aromas. When a wine is described as having a peardrop note, it means you get that candy-like pear fragrance (mixed with a bit of nail-polish/banana ester). It often indicates a young, carbonically fermented red or a specific ester presence, lending a playful, candied fruit element to the nose.
Pencil shavings: An aroma that combines cedar wood and graphite, much like the smell when you sharpen a pencil. It’s a mix of woody and mineral scents. In red wines (notably Bordeaux blends or Cabernet Franc), “pencil shavings” is a classic descriptor – it suggests a refined cedar note from oak and a graphite/mineral note likely from the wine’s structure. This aroma adds an elegant touch, evoking imagery of cedar cigar boxes and freshly sharpened pencils; it often signifies a certain pedigree in the wine and complements its fruit with a subtle woody-mineral complexity.
Peony: A floral aroma akin to peony flowers – which are rosy, soft, and slightly green in scent. Peony in wine is a delicate red floral note (less potent than rose, more so than violets). Some red wines, like certain Pinots or Beaujolais, can exhibit peony-like aromas, contributing a gentle, charming floral lift to the bouquet. It’s the kind of subtle flower scent that sits in the background, giving the wine a touch of elegance and prettiness without dominating its other aromas.
Perfumed: Describes a wine with highly aromatic, flower-like and sweet scents, as if one were smelling a fine perfume. A perfumed wine is very fragrant – often with abundant floral, spice, or exotic fruit notes that captivate the nose. For example, many aromatic whites (like Gewürztraminer or Moscato) are perfumed: they fill the glass with lush scents of rose, jasmine, lychee, etc. This term emphasizes the wine’s intense and pleasant aromatic qualities, suggesting complexity and allure in the bouquet, much like a well-crafted perfume.
Persimmon: A fruit note reminiscent of ripe persimmon, which has a sweet, mellow pumpkin-like flavor when fully ripe. The aroma is subtle, honeyed-orange and autumnal. In wine, persimmon might not be a common descriptor, but if used it indicates a gentle sweet-orange fruit tone with a bit of earthiness. It could appear in some rich whites or orange wines, adding a unique late-fall fruit character (imagine something between dried apricot and pumpkin flesh). It lends an interesting, soft fruit sweetness that’s a bit different from typical citrus or stone fruit notes.
Petrichor: The distinctive smell of rain on dry earth or pavement. It’s that fresh, mineral, slightly dusty scent that rises from the ground when rain first falls after a long dry spell. In wine, petrichor is used to describe a very fine-grained earthy minerality – as if the wine carries an essence of wet stones and soil after rain. This note can be found in some highly mineral wines (like certain Rieslings or Syrahs) and adds a nostalgic earth-scented freshness to the aroma, evoking imagery of thunderstorms and wet pebbles.
Petrol (kerosene): A unique aroma often found in aged Riesling, reminiscent of gasoline or kerosene. While it might sound odd, in appropriate measure it’s considered a positive varietal trait for mature Rieslings. The petrol note smells like the fumes of a gas heater or a fuel can – a mix of chemical, rubbery, and oily scents. It adds complexity to the wine’s bouquet, intertwining with the citrus and mineral notes typical of Riesling. In other wines, a strong petrol/kerosene smell could indicate a flaw or sunstruck wine, but in Riesling, aficionados often appreciate a hint of this distinctive aroma as part of the wine’s character.
Pine (resin): An aroma of pine wood or pine resin, evocative of a pine forest or sap. It’s woody, fresh, and slightly balsamic (think Christmas tree scent or resinous Greek retsina wine). In some wines, especially those aged in certain pine barrels or very herbal reds, you might get a pine-like note. It brings a cooling evergreen character, as if a dash of pine sap or needles were present. This can add an interesting resinous, herbal depth to the wine, although if too strong it can dominate the profile (as intentionally in retsina, which is explicitly resinated).
Pineapple: A tropical fruit aroma like that of ripe pineapple. It’s sweet, bright, and a bit tangy. In wine, pineapple notes often appear in warm-climate white wines or those that underwent certain fermentations (like some Chardonnays or Pinot Grigios), lending a juicy, tropical flair. This note makes the wine’s fruit profile feel exuberant and sun-kissed – akin to the smell of freshly cut pineapple chunks, complete with that balance of sugar and acidity. It adds an appealing lusciousness and is usually a sign of ripe grapes and a crowd-pleasing flavor.
Plum: A fruit note reminiscent of plums, usually meaning dark plums unless specified otherwise. It smells of sweet, ripe stone fruit with a bit of skin tartness. In many red wines (Merlot, Malbec, etc.), plum is a common descriptor, providing a sense of round, purple fruitiness. It can range from the lighter, fresher aroma of red plums to the deeper, jammy notes of black plums. Plum character in wine adds a friendly, rich fruit base – it’s less sharp than berry and less floral than cherry, giving a solid core of fruit that’s juicy and smooth.
Polished: Describes a wine that is smooth, refined, and well-crafted in texture and flavor. A polished wine has no rough edges – tannins are silky, acids are integrated, and flavors are harmonious. Tasting it is like feeling polished marble: seamless and sleek. This term often applies to higher-end wines where everything feels carefully resolved and elegant. The wine glides over the palate with grace, indicating quality and skilled winemaking, leaving an impression of finesse and completeness.
Pomegranate: A red fruit note akin to pomegranate seeds or juice. Pomegranate in wine brings a tart, tangy berry-cherry quality with a slight astringency (like the bitterness of the seeds). It’s both fruity and a touch floral. In tasting, citing pomegranate means the wine has bright red fruit characteristics that are a bit more tart or exotic than standard cherry or berry. It can be found in certain vibrant reds or rosés, adding a crisp, cranberry-like snap of flavor that keeps the wine feeling lively.
Potpourri: An aroma that smells like a mix of dried flowers, spices, and herbs – essentially the scent of potpourri. It’s complex: you might detect dried rose petals, lavender, maybe a touch of clove or citrus peel all together. In wine, a potpourri note indicates a heady, perfumed mix of dried floral and spice elements. This is often associated with nuanced, mature wines or certain Rhône reds (like aged Châteauneuf-du-Pape) that exhibit an array of sweet dried flower and herbal scents. It gives the wine a rich aromatic tapestry, reminiscent of sniffing a bowl of decorative dried flowers and spices.
Powerful: Describes a wine with very intense presence – whether through high alcohol, concentrated flavor, or both. A powerful wine immediately commands attention on the palate; it’s full-bodied, with robust flavors that might include dense fruit, oak, and ample tannin or warmth. This kind of wine can feel “big” and forceful, saturating your taste buds. It’s not subtle or delicate – instead it’s assertive, and you sense its strength in each sip. Such wines often benefit from hearty food pairings or aging to mellow their might.
