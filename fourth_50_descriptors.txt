Prune: An aroma of dried plums (prunes), which is deep, sweet, and slightly raisined. Prune notes often develop in wines made from very ripe grapes or wines that have aged a long time, especially fortified or warm-climate reds. It indicates the fresh plum has moved to a denser, sweeter profile with hints of fig and raisin. This adds a rich, mature fruit character to the wine, leaning towards the dessert fruit spectrum. While a bit of prune can give complexity, too much might suggest overripe or oxidized fruit, making the wine taste flat if freshness is lost.
Pungent: A general term meaning the wine has a very strong, intense smell that sharply catches your attention (not necessarily in a good way). A pungent aroma in wine could be something like a strong sulfur smell, an acrid vinegar note, or even an overwhelmingly green or spicy odor – anything that is penetrating and forceful to the nose. It often implies something is off or extreme: for example, a wine high in volatile acidity might have a pungent vinegar/solvent smell. Essentially, if a wine’s aroma is described as pungent, it’s powerful and often not very pleasant or refined, coming on too strong in one particular aspect (spice, acid, sulfur, etc.).
Quince: A fruit aroma similar to quince paste or cooked quince, which is like a combination of apple and pear but more aromatic and often detected in a jelly/jam form. Quince is tart and floral when fresh, but usually enjoyed cooked, which yields a honeyed, apple-pear marmalade scent. In wine, quince notes can appear in certain structured whites or aged sweet wines, adding an old-fashioned orchard fruit complexity. It gives the impression of a spiced pear/apple preserve – fragrant, slightly tangy-sweet, and exotic. Mentioning quince often highlights an elegant fruit nuance beyond common apples and pears.
Racy: Describes a wine with high, zingy acidity that gives it an exciting, brisk character. A racy wine tingles the palate; it’s vibrant and energetic due to that acidity, often coupled with pronounced minerality. Tasting a racy wine is like experiencing a bolt of lightning – it has focus and intensity, especially on the back of the tongue and cheeks (mouth-watering effect). This term is usually a compliment, suggesting the wine is lean, sharply defined, and lively (commonly used for Rieslings, Loire whites, etc.), providing a very clean and refreshing profile.
Raisin: The aroma or flavor of raisins (dried grapes) – sweet, concentrated, and a bit caramelized. In wine, raisin notes typically indicate very ripe grapes or partial drying (as in Amarone or dessert wines). It smells like a mix of grape candy and dried fruit sweetness. While small hints can add richness, a pronounced raisin character can mean the wine edges toward a fortified or oxidized style. On the palate it imparts sugary dried fruit tones and often a higher alcohol warmth. So, raisin contributes a deep, sweet, brown-ish fruitiness reminiscent of raisins or even dates, enhancing the wine’s complexity in certain styles.
Raspberry: A red fruit aroma akin to ripe raspberries. It’s bright, sweet-tart, and very fragrant – imagine the smell when you crush a handful of fresh raspberries. In many red wines (Pinot Noir, Grenache, etc.), raspberry notes are common, giving a youthful, juicy red berry character. It can range from the candy-like scent of raspberry jam (if very ripe) to the zesty freshness of just-picked berries. This note makes a wine’s fruit profile feel lively and charming, often balancing more serious flavors with its lighthearted, sweet red-fruit tone.
Red currant: An aroma of red currants – small, tart red berries. Red currant smells like a mix of cranberry and raspberry but more on the tart, earthy side. In wine, red currant notes often suggest a bright, slightly sour red fruit element, common in some cool-climate reds or Bordeaux blends. It gives a wine crisp fruitiness without too much sweetness, kind of a crunchy berry vibe. This contributes to complexity by adding a tangy fruit note that can counterbalance richer flavors.
Red plum: The aroma of red-fleshed plums, which are slightly lighter and more tart than dark plums. Think of the scent when biting into a red or yellow plum – sweet but with a fresh zing near the skin. In wine, red plum notes indicate a gentle, rounded fruit presence that isn’t overly deep or dried. It’s often found in Merlot or other medium-bodied reds, lending a smooth, easy fruit character. Compared to black plum (which is denser), red plum is a touch brighter and more delicate, adding to a wine’s approachability and soft fruit impression.
Rhubarb: A tart, vegetal-fruit aroma reminiscent of fresh rhubarb stalks or rhubarb pie. Rhubarb smells a bit like sour red berries crossed with green herbal tones – it’s distinctly sharp yet pleasing in a culinary way. In wine, a rhubarb note might appear in certain high-acid reds or rosés, giving a tangy red fruit and earthy green combo. It can make the wine interesting by adding that slightly sour, garden-vegetal hint (like the smell of cutting rhubarb). Often, rhubarb in wine points to a puckering, cool climate style with restrained sweetness and an edgy fruit profile.
Rich: Describes a wine with abundant flavor and texture, often leaning towards sweetness or ripeness. A rich wine fills your mouth with layers of flavor – whether it’s dense fruit, creamy oak, or high alcohol giving weight. It conveys a sense of fullness and generosity; the opposite of thin or austere. For instance, a rich Chardonnay might have bold tropical fruit, butter, and vanilla in spades. The term implies the wine is luxurious in mouthfeel and taste, delivering a lot of substance. Rich wines can be very satisfying, though they need balance to not feel cloying or heavy.
Robust: A term for a wine that is full-bodied, strong, and bursting with flavor – similar to calling it hearty or vigorous. A robust wine often has high alcohol or tannin, bold fruit, and possibly noticeable spice or oak. It stands up well to flavorful foods because it has its own intensity. Drinking a robust wine is a big experience: it’s not subtle or delicate, but rather assertive and muscle-bound (think robust like a strong handshake). This quality is often found in rustic or high-alcohol reds (like a rustic Italian red or a powerful Zin), which deliver a hearty punch on the palate.
Rose: A floral aroma of rose petals, often associated with aromatic grape varieties. It’s the classic sweet, soft smell of a blooming rose – perfumed and romantic. In wines like Gewürztraminer, Muscat, or even some Pinots, a rose note can be quite prominent, adding an elegant bouquet element. It makes the wine smell like a bouquet of roses was dipped into it. This aromatic note tends to indicate a very fragrant wine, contributing a sense of sweetness and luxury on the nose (even if the wine is dry).
Rosemary: An herbal aroma like that of fresh or dried rosemary. Rosemary has a strong, resinous pine-and-mint scent with a bit of woodiness. In wine, rosemary notes might appear in Mediterranean reds or those with a herbal garrigue profile, giving a savory, kitchen-herb character. It adds depth by bringing in that aromatic herb garden element – you smell hints of roast lamb herbs or focaccia seasoning. Rosemary in moderation can make a wine intriguing, lending a warming, earthy-herbal complexity to the flavor profile.
Rotten egg: An off-putting odor identical to the smell of a rotten egg, caused by hydrogen sulfide (H₂S) in the wine. This is a strong sulfuric stench – foul and unmistakable. If a wine smells of rotten eggs, it’s a flaw; likely the result of reductive winemaking issues. The aroma is extremely pungent (like sulfur or sewage) and overwhelms pleasant scents, rendering the wine essentially undrinkable until the issue is resolved (sometimes decanting can dissipate mild cases). In short, any hint of rotten egg means something went wrong in the bottle or barrel, as no healthy wine should carry that aroma.
Round: Describes a wine with a smooth, gentle mouthfeel and no harsh edges; it feels complete and satisfying on the palate. A round wine typically has moderate acidity and tannin that are well-integrated, giving a sense of softness. Flavors transition seamlessly. For example, a red wine with lush fruit and supple tannins can be called round if nothing juts out aggressively. It’s the kind of wine that glides rather than grips – very approachable and polished, often associated with a well-balanced, medium- to full-bodied style that caresses the mouth.
Saffron: A rare spice note reminiscent of saffron threads. Saffron has a distinctive warm, hay-like, slightly medicinal floral scent (famous in paella or risotto). In wine, saffron notes might appear in some aged sweet wines or whites, adding an exotic spice and earthy floral nuance. It’s subtle but complex – if you detect saffron, the wine likely has a layered bouquet. This aroma contributes a luxurious, spicy-floral touch that is quite unique, hinting at complexity akin to fine tea or spice markets.
Sage: An herbal aroma akin to sage leaves – earthy, camphorous, and savory-green. It’s the smell you get when you rub fresh sage: pungent and slightly peppery with a soft resinous quality. In wine, sage notes often surface in earthy reds or those from scrubland terroirs, infusing a wild-herb character. It gives the wine a savory dimension, as if a hint of dried sage or herbes de Provence is mixed in. This can complement the fruit with an intriguing herbal layer, enhancing complexity especially in Mediterranean-style wines.
Saline: A subtle aroma or taste of saltiness, like a sea breeze or saline solution. In certain wines (especially those from coastal vineyards or specific soils), you might perceive a briny note that isn’t literally salty to taste but gives an impression of salt air or ocean spray. We call that saline. On the palate it can also create a slight salivating effect. This characteristic adds crispness and can make a wine feel refreshing – imagine the faint saltiness you sense when near the sea. It often accompanies mineral profiles, enhancing the wine’s complexity with a delicate savory note.
Savory: Describes flavors or aromas that are not sweet but more aligned with herbs, umami, and spice – like broth, soy, grilled meat, or herbs. A savory wine emphasizes earth, spice, and non-fruit elements (think black olive, tobacco, leather, dried herbs) rather than overt fruity sweetness. Tasting a savory wine can remind you of food-like qualities (soy sauce, roasted game, tomato, etc.). This trait often appears in certain Old World reds or aged wines, making them pair exceptionally well with food since they bring their own seasoning-like complexity.
Sharp: An adjective for a wine that has a pronounced, pointed bite – usually from high acidity or occasionally very high alcohol (in a hot sense). A sharp wine comes across as piercing or edgy on the palate. For instance, a wine with sharp acidity will immediately cause a tangy, almost sour jolt to your tongue (imagine under-ripe citrus). If describing aroma, sharp could also mean a pungent note like vinegar or solvent is evident. Overall, calling a wine sharp implies it’s a bit aggressive in one aspect, lacking smoothness and potentially in need of more balance or age to mellow out.
Silky: Describes an exceptionally smooth, fine texture in a wine, especially with reference to tannins. A silky wine glides over the palate like silk – it’s soft, delicate, and polished. The mouthfeel is luxurious, with tannins that are present but feel very fine-grained and gentle. Often used for well-aged reds or impeccably balanced wines, silky indicates a high quality of texture. Flavor-wise, everything in a silky wine seems harmonious and refined, with nothing coarse or out of place as you taste it.
Slate: A mineral aroma or taste associated with slate rocks (often noted in wines from slate-rich soils, like Mosel Riesling). It smells like wet stones or pencil lead, giving a steely, earthy impression. Slate isn’t a smell of fruit or flowers; it’s almost like sniffing a piece of cold stone – subtle but distinct once you recognize it. In wine, a slate note contributes to the perception of minerality – a dry, flinty character that can balance out fruitiness. It’s usually a sign of a precise, terroir-driven wine, adding depth and a sense of place (like the craggy slate slopes where some vines grow).
Smoky: An aroma that suggests smoke or burnt wood. This can come from oak barrel toasting or sometimes from specific terroirs or even smoke exposure. A smoky note in wine might smell like a faint campfire, charred embers, or toasted bread. It adds a warm, rustic character. For example, some Syrahs have a lovely smoky/bacon aroma, or an oaked Chardonnay might carry a whiff of smoke from the barrel char. This note, when balanced, can lend complexity and an almost grilling-like savory element. However, if excessive (like in a wine affected by wildfire smoke), it can dominate unpleasantly, tasting ashy.
Smooth: Refers to a wine with a pleasing, easy texture and well-integrated flavors/tannins. A smooth wine goes down effortlessly; nothing harsh or bitter catches your palate. The tannins (if a red) are soft and velvety, the acids are gentle, and the wine feels round. Often used for reds that are mellow or whites that are creamy, smooth is generally positive, implying the wine is polished and approachable. It may not always mean the wine is very complex, but it does mean you won’t be jangled by rough edges – it’s a comfortable, satisfying drink.
Soapy: An unusual aroma that can resemble scented soap or perfumed soap suds. It’s somewhat floral and waxy but with a cleansing-product quality. A soapy note in wine often comes from high levels of certain aldehydes or improper clarification, and it’s typically considered a fault or at least odd. The smell might remind one of lavender soap or laundry soap, giving the wine a perfumey, slick aroma that doesn’t belong. It can also slightly affect taste, making it feel slick or chemically floral. A faint hint might be overshadowed by other notes, but if you distinctly think “soap,” the wine likely has an imbalance or minor flaw in its aroma profile.
Soft: Indicates a wine with low acidity and/or gentle tannins, yielding a mellow, plush mouthfeel. A soft wine won’t poke or prod your palate – it’s kind and yielding in structure. The term is often applied to easy-drinking wines or those with a bit of sweetness that round out any edges. For example, a soft red may have mild tannin and lower acid so it feels smooth and cushioned. Soft doesn’t mean flavorless; the wine can be fruity and full, but everything about it is undemanding on your mouth. It’s the opposite of sharp or harsh – instead, think smooth, round, and cuddly in wine form.
Sour cherry: The aroma or taste of tart red cherries (like Morello cherries used in baking). Sour cherry is bright and tangy, less sweet than regular cherry, with a slight pucker. In wine, especially some Italian reds (like Sangiovese-based wines), sour cherry notes are classic – they give a high-toned red fruit brightness and a bit of pleasant bitterness. This flavor lifts the wine, making it mouth-watering and adding complexity alongside sweeter fruit notes. It’s as if a touch of cherry skin sourness or a dash of cherry pit bitterness accompanies the fruit, which can be very appetizing and food-friendly.
Spicy: Having noticeable spice-like aromas or flavors, akin to spices used in cooking or baking. This is a broad descriptor – a spicy wine might exhibit black pepper, cloves, cinnamon, anise, ginger, or chili-like heat in its profile. It doesn’t mean the wine literally contains spices, but compounds from oak or grape skins can mimic those sensations. For example, a Zinfandel could be called spicy if it has peppery and cinnamon notes. Spicy gives the wine a lively kick beyond fruit: it tingles the senses a bit, much as actual spices do in food, and adds excitement and complexity to the aroma/flavor.
Stalky: An aroma or taste that suggests grape stems or green twigs. It’s a green, slightly woody note often from wines that had significant stem contact (like whole-cluster fermentations). Stalky can manifest as a kind of bitter green wood or vegetal flavor – imagine chewing on a grape stem or a bit of bark. In moderation, it can contribute structure or an intriguing herbal character, but if too pronounced it can make the wine seem under-ripe or astringent. It’s generally a term you’d use when a wine’s tannin or flavor has that raw plant quality, hinting at stems or seeds.
Stewed fruit: A flavor and aroma profile that suggests fruits have been cooked down, like in a compote or stew. Instead of bright, fresh fruit, you get soft, mixed fruit notes often with a slightly caramelized or jammy edge – like the filling of a pie or a pot of mixed berries simmered with sugar. In a wine, stewed fruit indicates very ripe fruit that’s lost some definition (common in hot-climate or overripe wines). You might detect something akin to stewed plums, cooked strawberries, or syrupy berry mix. This gives the wine a rich, homogenous fruit sweetness but can also mean it’s low in freshness – the opposite of vibrant or crisp fruit. In balanced measure it can be homely and pleasant, but too much stewed character might suggest the wine is flabby or made from overripe grapes.
Strawberry: A red fruit aroma like that of ripe strawberries. It’s sweet, aromatic, and friendly – think of the smell when you bite into a fresh strawberry on a summer day, or even strawberry jam for a sweeter version. This note is common in many rosés and lighter reds (like Pinot Noir or Beaujolais), lending a charming, easy fruitiness. It can range from fresh wild strawberry (more delicate and perfumed) to candied strawberry (like in some youthful wines). The presence of strawberry makes a wine feel approachable and youthful, and often it’s one of multiple red fruit notes that together shape the wine’s fruity profile.
Sun-dried tomato: A savory aroma reminiscent of tomatoes that have been dried under the sun (concentrating their flavor). It smells like intense, slightly sweet tomato with an herbal, almost leathery edge. In certain wines (occasionally in some Italian reds or warm-climate Syrahs), a sun-dried tomato note can appear, giving a tangy, umami-rich nuance. This is different from fresh tomato leaf or green tomato – it’s more about the dried, slightly smoky tomato skin. It adds a rustic, Mediterranean touch to a wine’s aroma, bridging the gap between fruity and savory.
Supple: Describes a wine with a smooth, pliant texture and gentle, well-resolved tannins. A supple wine feels caressing on the palate – there is structure, but it’s flexible and yielding rather than firm. It’s often used for red wines whose tannins have softened (either through aging or good ripeness) so that they provide body without roughness. Flavors in a supple wine flow easily; nothing is awkward or stiff. In essence, to call a wine supple is to praise how it moves in your mouth – gracefully and without resistance, offering richness and softness at the same time.
Sweet: Having a noticeable level of sugar-derived sweetness on the palate. A sweet wine tastes sugary, like desserts or ripe fruit syrup – common in wines like Sauternes, Port, or late-harvest Riesling. The term can also describe aromas that give an illusion of sweetness (such as very ripe fruit, vanilla, or honey notes), even if the wine is technically dry. When you say a wine is sweet without qualification, you usually mean it contains residual sugar that you can clearly taste (as opposed to a dry wine). The sweetness often brings flavors of honey, candied fruit, or sugar-coated nuts, and contributes a viscous, full body. Balance is key: a sweet wine should also have acidity to avoid being cloying.
Tannic: Describes a wine high in tannins – those compounds mostly from grape skins, seeds, and oak that create a drying, astringent sensation. A tannic wine will make your mouth feel somewhat dry or rough, similar to strong black tea. The term often applies to young red wines (like Cabernet Sauvignon or Nebbiolo) that have a big structure. Tannins themselves don’t have a strong flavor; it’s more of a textural and slightly bitter sensation. Saying a wine is tannic means this drying effect is a dominant characteristic at its current state. Over time or with food, tannins can soften. While tannins provide backbone and aging potential, a very tannic wine when young can feel harsh – the key is that the fruit and other elements catch up to integrate with the tannin.
Tar: An aroma reminiscent of tar or asphalt, often noted in certain robust red wines (famously in Barolo, which is described as “tar and roses”). It’s a dense, smoky, resinous smell – think of freshly paved road or roofing tar. In wine, a tar note provides a deep, dark counterpoint to fruit and floral elements. It might seem odd, but in small amounts it adds intrigue and complexity. The presence of tar typically signals a wine with serious structure and intensity. On the palate, one doesn’t exactly “taste tar,” but you can get a sense of that chewy, smoky character intertwined with bold fruit and earth. It’s generally a positive descriptor in the right context, indicating a powerful, evocative aroma.
Tart: A taste sensation of high acidity that is sour or puckering, similar to unripe fruit or lemon juice. If a wine is described as tart, it means it leans towards sharp acidity in its flavor profile – imagine biting a cranberry or a green apple. Tartness can be refreshing and make a wine food-friendly, but if overwhelming it might feel unbalanced. Some wines are intentionally crafted to be a bit tart (like certain light whites or rosés) to emphasize brightness. Essentially, calling a wine tart highlights its sour-acidic snap that can enliven the palate, though one would hope it’s matched by enough fruit or sweetness so it doesn’t become too austere.
Thin: Describes a wine that feels light-bodied and lacking in flavor intensity or concentration. A thin wine might taste diluted, watery or simply under-fruited – as if it’s missing the mid-palate weight. It often comes paired with high acid, making it feel more like flavored water than rich wine. The term is usually negative, suggesting the wine doesn’t carry enough substance (ripe fruit, glycerin, etc.) to be satisfying. For instance, an over-cropped or underripe wine might come off as thin if the grapes didn’t have much to give. Such a wine may have some aroma and flavor, but they are fleeting, and the overall impression is of weakness or hollowness on the palate.
Thyme: An herbal aroma like fresh or dried thyme leaves. Thyme has a savory, slightly minty and lemony herb scent that is prominent in Mediterranean cooking. In wine, especially those from regions with wild herbs (think Provence or parts of Spain/Italy), a thyme note can surface, adding a green, earthy spiciness. It contributes to the wine’s complexity by layering in that dried herbal character – as if a sprig of thyme had been steeped in it. Along with rosemary, bay, etc., thyme falls under the garrigue family of notes, painting a picture of shrubby hillsides and adding a pleasant savory quality.
Tight: Describes a wine that is closed in and not expressing much aroma or flavor, often due to youth. A tight wine feels constricted – you sense the structure (acid/tannins) more than the actual flavors. It’s like the wine is holding back. On the palate, a tight wine might show some promise but everything is compact and not giving. This is common in young high-end wines that need time or air. Calling a wine tight typically suggests that if you decant or age it, it should “loosen up” and reveal more. In the moment, though, it means you have to search for the aromas/flavors because they aren’t readily apparent or they feel bound up by the wine’s structure.
Toast (toasted bread): An aroma of toasted bread or lightly charred toast, usually a sign of oak barrel influence (from the toasting of barrel staves) or yeast aging. It smells like the crust of freshly toasted baguette – warm, grainy, and slightly smoky. In sparkling wines, a toasty note is highly prized, coming from time on lees and resembling brioche or toast. In still wines, the term might indicate a light char from the oak (complementing vanilla and spice tones). This note adds a comforting, breakfasty warmth to the wine’s nose, rounding out fruit with a subtle roasty nuance that can be quite inviting.
Tobacco: An aroma reminiscent of cured tobacco leaves or a freshly opened cigar box. It’s a warm, woody, slightly sweet dried-leaf smell with a hint of smokiness. Many complex red wines (like Cabernet, Bordeaux blends, or Tempranillo) develop a tobacco note, especially with some age. It adds a sophisticated, earthy layer to the bouquet, as if you stepped into a humidor. The scent can range from fresh pipe tobacco (more sweet and aromatic) to drier cigar-box cedar. In any case, it’s generally seen as a positive trait in moderation, contributing to the wine’s savory and tertiary aromatic profile.
Toffee: A sweet, caramelized aroma reminiscent of buttery toffee candy or caramel fudge. It combines the smell of burnt sugar and butter – rich and decadent. In wine, toffee notes can appear in well-oaked whites, some dessert wines, or aged wines that develop those caramel tones. It indicates a certain sweetness (even if the wine is dry) and oak influence, as vanilla and caramel from oak can merge into a toffee impression. This adds a dessert-like charm to the aroma, often accompanying notes of vanilla, butterscotch, or coffee. Essentially, it’s that creamy burnt sugar scent that can make a wine smell luxurious and indulgent.
Tomato: An aroma or flavor note reminiscent of tomatoes – usually more like the green stem or a just-cut tomato than the cooked kind. In some wines, especially certain Italian reds or occasionally Sauvignon Blancs, you might get a faint note of tomato skin or fresh tomato flesh, which is a slightly sweet-green smell. It can lend an interesting rustic or vegetal nuance. However, if someone says a wine tastes like tomato juice or sauce (more common in flawed or very oxidized wines), that’s usually not a good thing. Generally, a subtle tomato leaf or tomato flesh note adds to a wine’s herbaceous complexity, tying in with other green or earthy elements.
Tomato leaf: A specific green, aromatic note identical to the smell you get when rubbing tomato plant leaves. It’s very pungent-green, with a sharp yet fresh garden scent. In wine, tomato leaf is most famously a descriptor for some styles of Sauvignon Blanc or Cabernet Franc, indicating pronounced pyrazines (the compounds responsible for herbaceous aromas). It gives a wine a distinctive vegetative character – like sniffing a tomato vine on a warm day. This can be appealing if balanced, contributing to a fresh, herbaceous profile that adds dimension, but if too dominant it may make the wine smell overly green or unripe.
Truffle: An earthy, pungent aroma reminiscent of truffle fungus (the gourmet underground mushrooms). Truffles have a very distinctive scent – musky, garlicky, and soil-like all at once, considered highly complex and alluring. In certain fine aged wines (especially Burgundies, Barolos, etc.), a truffle note can emerge, giving the wine a luxurious earthy savoriness. It’s often described as earthy mushroom with a touch of garlic and forest floor. This aroma adds immense complexity and is usually seen as a sign of a wine’s maturation and high pedigree. It contributes a deep umami character that plays beautifully with savory foods.
Underripe: Describes flavors or aromas that indicate the grapes were not fully ripe when picked. An underripe wine often has green, vegetal, or excessively tart elements – for example, green bell pepper, grass, or very sour fruit notes that lack sweetness. The tannins might also be harsh because they didn’t soften on the vine. If someone calls a wine underripe, they mean it tastes a bit raw or thin, with fruit that’s more acid-driven and possibly herbal to the point of imbalance. Think of biting into an under-ripe plum – it’s hard, green-tasting, and not very aromatic. In wine, that translates to a lack of depth and a prominence of green flavors.
Vanilla: A sweet baking aroma derived largely from oak aging. Vanilla in wine smells like vanilla extract or vanilla bean – warm, creamy, and dessert-like. New oak barrels (especially those made from American oak) impart vanillin compounds, giving wines a distinctive vanilla note often noticeable in Chardonnays, Rioja, or Cabernet aged in oak. This note can add a sense of sweetness even if the wine is dry, complementing fruit flavors with a custardy touch. It’s generally a welcome aroma when balanced, contributing to the complexity and “polish” of a wine’s bouquet, much like vanilla is used to enhance desserts.
Vegetal: A term for aromas/flavors that are vegetable-like or green plant-like. A vegetal wine might remind one of green beans, bell peppers, cabbage or stems – essentially notes that are more garden than orchard. This often comes from unripe grapes or certain grape varieties with high methoxypyrazines. In moderation, light vegetal hints (like cut grass, bell pepper) can add complexity, but vegetal usually suggests it’s a bit too pronounced, making the wine smell or taste more like veggies than fruit. For example, a red wine with strong vegetal character might not have enough ripe fruit to balance its green pepper or bean pod flavors, coming off as under-ripe or rustic.
Velvety: Describes a texture that is smooth and plush, much like the feel of velvet fabric. A velvety wine has soft, fine tannins that coat the mouth gently, and a rich body that gives a sense of fullness without harshness. When you sip it, the wine flows easily and feels luxurious on the tongue – structured yet utterly smooth. This term is often applied to well-aged reds or exceptionally balanced young ones where tannins are present but very refined. It implies both high quality and immediate pleasure; a velvety wine is polished, caressing, and leaves a lingering, gentle finish. It’s a step beyond “smooth,” suggesting an almost tangible plushness in the wine’s mouthfeel.
