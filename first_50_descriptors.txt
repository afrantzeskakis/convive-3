Acacia: A delicate floral aroma reminiscent of acacia blossoms, which are sweet and lightly fragrant. In wine, acacia notes present as subtle, honeyed flower scents with a hint of fresh greenery. This aroma gives the impression of gentle white flowers and adds a soft floral character to the wine’s bouquet.
Acidic: Describes a sour or tart taste in the wine, caused by higher acidity. An acidic wine makes your mouth water and can evoke sensations similar to biting into a lemon or green apple. It contributes freshness and vibrancy to the flavor, though if too acidic it may taste sharp or sour.
Aftertaste: The flavor impressions that remain on the palate after you have swallowed the wine. Also called the finish, this describes the lingering tastes (such as fruit, oak, or spice) that you continue to perceive. A pleasant, long aftertaste (long finish) is usually a sign of quality, whereas a short or harsh aftertaste might be less desirable.
Alcoholic: A term for a wine whose alcohol is out of balance, resulting in a noticeable burn or warmth. On the nose and palate it may smell or taste hot, like higher-proof spirits, and can give a slight sting in the back of the throat. An alcoholic wine often feels weighty and may overwhelm more delicate flavors.
Allspice: A spice note in wine reminiscent of the sweet and warm aroma of allspice berries. It smells like a mix of clove, cinnamon, and nutmeg combined (much like the allspice seasoning). In wine tasting, allspice can present as a subtle baking spice complexity, adding depth to the aroma profile.
Almond: A nutty aroma or flavor suggestive of almonds, which can be either raw and slightly bitter or sweet like marzipan. In some wines you may detect the scent of crushed almond skin or almond extract, giving a mild nuttiness. This note is especially common in certain whites and aged sparkling wines, contributing a subtle, sweet-nut nuance.
Angular: Describes a wine with hard, sharp-feeling edges, often due to prominent acidity or tannin. An angular wine isn’t round or smooth – instead it presents its components in a pointed way, which can make the flavors feel less integrated. The taste comes across as brisk or austere, lacking a sense of softness on the palate.
Anise: A spice note smelling of licorice or fennel seed, similar to the aroma of star anise. In wine, anise often appears as a faint sweet-herbal character, adding complexity. It might remind the taster of licorice candies or the subtle scent of fennel, contributing a mild spicy sweetness to the wine’s profile.
Apple (baked): An aroma of cooked or baked apples, as you would find in apple pie or cider. This note suggests sweet, warm apple compote with hints of caramelization. In wine, a baked apple flavor often indicates very ripe fruit or certain aging processes, giving a rich, comforting orchard-fruit character.
Apple (green): The scent or tart taste of green apples (like Granny Smith). It’s a crisp, fresh fruit note with high acidity, reminiscent of cut green apple or apple skins. This flavor makes a wine taste zesty and mouth-watering, contributing to a refreshing, fruity sharpness.
Apple (ripe): The aroma of ripe, fresh apples (red or yellow apples). This note is like biting into a sweet, juicy apple – fruity and slightly sweet but with less tartness than green apple. Ripe apple notes add an orchard-fresh, wholesome fruit character to the wine’s profile.
Apricot (dried): A concentrated sweet fruit aroma akin to dried apricots. This note has a rich, candy-like apricot scent with hints of honey and syrup because the fruit’s sugars are concentrated by drying. It gives wines a deep, lush stone-fruit flavor that is sweeter and more caramelized than fresh apricot.
Apricot (ripe): The aroma of fresh, ripe apricots, a soft and sweet stone-fruit note. It smells like a juicy apricot at peak ripeness – fruity and floral with mild sweetness. Ripe apricot notes in wine lend a gentle, sunny fruit flavor often found in certain white wines (like Viognier or some dessert wines).
Aromatic: A term indicating a wine with pronounced and intense aromas. An aromatic wine is very fragrant – when you smell it, a variety of scents (floral, fruity, spicy, etc.) are immediately evident and expressive. This quality is typical of certain grape varieties (like Gewürztraminer or Riesling) known for their strong, perfumed scent.
Ashy: A smell or taste reminiscent of ash or soot, as if from a fireplace. An ashy note can make a wine’s aroma seem dry or smoky, like extinguished charcoal or cigarette ash. This character might come from heavy toasting of barrels or smoke influence and can add a rustic, smoky dimension to the wine’s profile.
Asparagus: A green, vegetal aroma similar to fresh asparagus spears. This note is herbaceous and can be encountered in certain wines (notably some Sauvignon Blancs) where it gives a green vegetable scent. Depending on intensity, asparagus notes can add complexity (subtle fresh green nuance) or be considered off-putting if too pronounced (canned asparagus tone).
Astringent: Causing a drying, puckering sensation on the palate, usually due to high tannins. An astringent wine makes your mouth feel rough or dry, similar to strong black tea or an unripe persimmon. This tactile feeling is not a flavor per se, but it affects the taste experience by making the wine seem firm, and if excessive can be unpleasantly bitter or drying.
Austere: Describes a wine that is hard, unsmiling, or lacking obvious fruit sweetness – often due to high acid or tannin and minimal ripe fruit flavors. An austere wine feels strict on the palate: it may taste lean, with more mineral, acid, or bitter elements and very little plushness. Though it might soften with age or air, in its youth an austere wine comes across as reserved and not immediately generous in flavor.
Autolytic: Refers to aromas derived from yeast breakdown (autolysis), often encountered in sparkling wines or wines aged on lees. An autolytic aroma is characterized by notes like bread dough, brioche, biscuit, or yeasty florals. It can give the wine a toasty, bakery-like scent (think of fresh bread or pastry), adding complexity beyond fruit characters.
Backward: A term for a wine that is closed or not showing much of its character, often due to youth. A backward wine keeps its aromas and flavors hidden – you might detect structure (acid, tannin) more than fruit or bouquet. It suggests the wine needs more time (aging or air) to “come forward” and express its full range of flavors.
Bacon fat: A rich, savory aroma reminiscent of smoked bacon or cured meats. In certain red wines (like Syrah/Shiraz), you might notice this meaty, smoky-fat scent intermingled with the fruit. It gives the wine a savory depth, as if there’s a faint note of crispy bacon or ham adding complexity to the nose and palate.
Baked: A term indicating flavors of stewed or overripe fruit, as if the wine’s fruit component has been baked by heat. A baked wine might taste of cooked berries or prunes rather than fresh fruit, often accompanied by higher alcohol. It can suggest the grapes came from a very hot climate or were sun-exposed post-harvest, yielding a jammy, stewed fruit character with possibly a slight raisiny note.
Balanced: Describes a wine in which all major components (fruit flavor, acidity, tannin, alcohol, sweetness, etc.) are in harmony. In a balanced wine, no single element sticks out harshly – for example, it doesn’t taste overly acidic, too alcoholic, or too tannic. The flavors and sensations complement each other, resulting in a smooth, cohesive tasting experience where the wine feels “just right” on the palate.
Balsamic: An aromatic note that calls to mind balsamic vinegar or aromatic resins – a sweet-sour, woody scent. In wine, “balsamic” often refers to a complex mix of dried herbs, aged wood, and a slight vinegar-like sweetness (in a positive sense). It can give an impression of depth, like aged balsam or aromatic woods, adding intrigue with nuances of spice and tart sweetness (commonly found in aged Italian reds).
Banana: A fruity aroma reminiscent of bananas, often specifically the smell of artificial banana candy or very ripe banana. This note is common in some young wines (like certain Beaujolais Nouveau) due to fermentation esters. It gives the wine a distinctly sweet, tropical fruit tone, sometimes described as smelling like banana Runts or banana taffy.
Band-Aid: A term for a particular medicinal/plastic aroma in wine, resembling the smell of adhesive bandages. It’s a kind of phenolic or chemical scent often associated with Brettanomyces yeast influence. The Band-Aid note comes across as an artificial, antiseptic smell (some say like a mix of plastic and iodine) and is usually considered a flaw when noticeable, as it can mask the wine’s fruit.
Barnyard: An earthy, farm-like smell that can appear in some wines, often linked to Brettanomyces yeast. “Barnyard” evokes the aroma of a barn – think of hay, dirt, and a bit of animal musk or manure. While it sounds unpleasant, in small amounts it can add rustic complexity (like a faint horse stable or farm straw scent), though if strong it’s usually seen as a fault making the wine smell dirty or funky.
Basil: A subtle herbal note reminiscent of fresh basil leaves. This aroma is green, slightly sweet, and peppery (basil is an aromatic herb used in cooking). In a wine context, a hint of basil might show up alongside other green herbal tones, contributing a fresh garden-herb character that can make the wine’s aroma profile more savory and complex.
Bay leaf: An herbal aroma akin to dried bay laurel leaves. It comes across as a subtle woody herb scent with notes of menthol and spice. In wine, bay leaf nuances add a dried-herb complexity (often found in some reds), bringing to mind the smell of a spice cupboard or herbal tea and lending a savory, aromatic quality to the flavor.
Beeswax: A waxy, honeycomb-like aroma that combines elements of honey, wax, and floral pollen. It smells a bit sweet and resinous, much like a honeycomb or a beeswax candle. In wines (especially some whites or wines affected by noble rot), beeswax notes contribute a rich, waxy-honey scent that adds depth and is often associated with texture as well.
Beetroot (red beet): An earthy-sweet aroma similar to fresh red beets. This note smells a bit like soil and sweet root vegetables – an earthy, slightly rustic sweetness. When a wine has a beetroot note, it often gives a vegetal earthiness (sometimes noted in certain Pinot Noirs or other earthy reds) that can remind one of fresh beets or beet juice.
Big: Describes a wine with bold, intense flavor or high body/alcohol that makes a strong impression. A “big” wine is full-bodied and rich; it might have high alcohol that gives warmth, and concentrated fruit or oak flavors that dominate the palate. Everything about it is ample – from aroma to taste, it’s powerful and not subtle.
Biscuit: A subtle bready aroma reminiscent of plain biscuits or crackers. Often encountered in traditional method sparkling wines, it smells like a mix of baked dough and slight butteriness, though not sweet like a cookie. The biscuit note suggests a wine has spent time aging on yeast lees, imparting that toasty, cracker-like nuance that adds complexity beyond fruit notes.
Bitter: A basic taste sensation typified by many tannic red wines or certain greens; it’s an unpleasant sharpness on the palate akin to unsweetened cocoa, black coffee, or citrus pith. In wine, bitterness often comes from tannins or unripe components, leaving a slight bitter aftertaste. While a hint of bitterness can contribute to structure, if it’s too pronounced the wine might taste harsh or astringent.
Black cherry: A deep, dark cherry aroma, like the scent of sweet black cherries or black cherry juice. This note is richer and slightly sweeter than red cherry, often found in fuller-bodied red wines (like Cabernet Sauvignon or Syrah). Black cherry gives a wine a lush, dark fruit character – think of ripe Bing cherries – contributing to a sense of sweetness and depth in the fruit profile.
Black pepper: A spicy aroma reminiscent of freshly cracked black peppercorns. It has a sharp, pungent spice scent that tingles the nose slightly, much like seasoning with pepper. In wine, a black pepper note is common in varieties like Syrah or Grüner Veltliner, adding a savory spice kick that complements the fruit with a peppery warmth on the nose and palate.
Black tea: An aroma or taste analogous to brewed black tea or tea leaves. It carries a mild bitterness and leafy, dry quality, sometimes with a subtle woody or floral undertone like certain teas. In wine, a black tea note often comes from tannins or evolution of flavors (common in some aged reds), lending a slight astringency and refined, leafy nuance to the flavor profile.
Blackberry: The scent of ripe blackberries – a dark berry aroma that is sweet and slightly tangy. It suggests juicy, wild blackberries bursting with flavor. This note is common in many red wines (like Zinfandel or Malbec), giving them a rich berry character; it adds to the perception of ripe, dark fruit sweetness in the wine.
Blackcurrant (cassis): The distinctive aroma of blackcurrant berries, which is deep, dark, and sweet-tart. It’s often likened to the smell of crème de cassis (blackcurrant liqueur) – concentrated and rich. Blackcurrant notes (commonly found in Cabernet Sauvignon) give wine a powerful dark fruit character, somewhat sharp yet sweet, anchoring the fruit profile with a recognizable currant scent.
Blueberry: A fruity aroma reminiscent of blueberries – sweet, with a slight tartness and a darker berry tone. In wine, blueberry notes often show up in certain red varietals (like Malbec or some Syrahs), contributing a plush, juicy berry character. It smells like ripe summer blueberries, adding to the perception of sweetness and round fruit flavor in the wine.
Body: The sense of weight or fullness of the wine in your mouth. Body isn’t a flavor but a structural impression – a light-bodied wine feels thin and watery (like skim milk), a full-bodied wine feels heavy and rich (like whole milk). Wines with more alcohol, glycerol, or extract have more body, giving a mouth-coating, substantial feel, whereas lighter-bodied wines feel more delicate and airy on the palate.
Boiled cabbage: A cooked vegetal aroma reminiscent of cabbage that has been boiled. This smell is sulfurous and cabbage-like (think of overcooked cruciferous vegetables). In wine, a boiled cabbage note is usually considered a flaw, often related to certain sulfur compounds or faults, giving the wine an unpleasant cooked veggie odor that detracts from cleaner aromas.
Boiled egg: An aroma reminiscent of a hard-boiled egg or the sulfurous smell of egg yolk. This is a reductive note typically caused by sulfur compounds like hydrogen sulfide. In wine, a boiled egg smell is considered a fault – it presents as a mild rotten egg or eggy odor, indicating the wine might have had low oxygen during winemaking, and it’s generally undesirable.
Boxwood: A pungent green aroma similar to crushed boxwood leaves or currant buds. It has a strong, catty, resinous smell (often likened to the scent of cat pee or tomcat urine in a more polite term). Boxwood is commonly associated with certain Sauvignon Blanc wines, giving them a sharp, green, musky-herbal note that can be intense and signature to that style.
Boysenberry: A berry aroma resembling boysenberries, which are a cross of blackberry, raspberry, and loganberry. It smells like a mix of sweet dark berry and bright red berry notes – juicy and ripe. In wine, boysenberry can describe a composite berry impression (often in fruit-forward reds) where you sense both the richness of blackberry and the tartness of raspberry together, contributing to a lush fruitiness.
Brioche: A rich bakery aroma reminiscent of buttery brioche bread. This note is bready, slightly sweet, and buttery, much like the scent of a freshly baked French brioche or pastry. It’s typically found in sparkling wines or certain whites aged on lees, where yeast contact imparts that toasty, bread-like fragrance, adding an elegant pastry nuance to the wine’s aroma.
Brooding: Describes a wine with deep, dark aromas and flavors that feel intense but somewhat closed or mysterious. A brooding wine often has significant dark fruit, earth, or spice notes that suggest depth, yet it doesn’t reveal everything at once. On the nose and palate it comes across as serious, perhaps a bit reserved or “inward,” with an impression that there’s more to unfurl given time or aging.
Bubblegum: A candied, sweet aroma similar to pink bubblegum. It carries a tutti-frutti or banana-strawberry candy scent that is distinctly confectionary. This note appears in some young wines made by carbonic maceration (like certain Beaujolais Nouveau), giving them a fun, candy-like fruitiness – as if one popped a piece of Bazooka or Hubba Bubba gum in the glass.
Burnt rubber: A distinctive smell of scorched or burnt rubber, like a hot rubber hose or tire. In wine, this is usually considered a fault or at least an odd trait, often noted in certain wines as a result of specific winemaking issues or grape characteristics (famously sometimes in South African Pinotage). It gives a wine a charred, acrid rubbery note that can mask more pleasant aromas if strong.
Butterscotch: A rich sweet aroma reminiscent of butterscotch candy or buttery caramel. It smells like melted brown sugar and butter combined – creamy, vanilla-like, and caramelized. In wine, butterscotch notes can develop in some oak-aged whites or aged dessert wines, lending a dessert-like profile (hints of toffee, caramel, butter) that adds to the wine’s complexity and sweetness impression.
